# Examples — what a session looks like

These are real, unedited outputs from the skills in this catalog. As you read, watch for
the thing that never happens: **not one line of the writer's prose gets rewritten.** Every
skill diagnoses, names the craft, and points at the fix — and leaves the writing to you.

You never need to prompt with precision. A rough *"here's a thing, it feels off"* is a
complete way to start — the skill runs the intake for you. Each skill also carries its own
**`## Try it`** section with more openers.

## dialogue-gym — diagnosing wooden dialogue

**The writer pasted a scene and asked what was wrong:**

> Hey, my dialogue keeps coming out wooden and I can't figure out why. Here's a scene
> between a teenage girl and her dad after he missed her piano recital — can you tell me
> what's going wrong and set me a drill?
>
> "I'm really angry at you right now, Dad, because you missed my recital," Sarah said
> angrily. "I know you're angry, and I feel guilty about missing your recital, because I
> was stuck at work," her father replied. "As you know, I've been practicing that piano
> piece for three months for this recital," Sarah said. "Yes, and as your father, I
> should have been there to support you, but my job as an accountant kept me late," he
> said. "I just want you to care about my music more than your spreadsheets," Sarah said.
> "You're right. From now on, I will prioritize your happiness over my career," her
> father said.

**The coach's reply** — it names the faults, sets a drill, and writes none of the lines:

Good — "wooden" is the right word for it, and the scene tells me exactly why. Before we start, here's how the gym works: I give you a short brief on one piece of craft, set a single tight drill with a hard cap, you write it and paste it back, and I give you a scored read — then the next drill, aimed at whatever you just showed. You bring the writing. I never write the lines for you, not even to demonstrate.

One calibration question first, because it changes how I'd grade: I'm assuming you want the hurt to live *under* these lines rather than get announced out loud. If you actually wanted it flat and blunt — a kid who really does just yell the literal thing — say so and I'll grade to that instead. I'll proceed on the subtext assumption.

Here's what's going wrong, deepest first.

**The engine is missing.** Read it for who wants what. Sarah wants to be cared about; Dad wants to agree with her. He concedes on line 2 ("I feel guilty"), concedes again on 4, and fully surrenders on 6 ("From now on, I will prioritize your happiness over my career"). Nobody pushes back. Two people agreeing isn't a scene yet — there's no clash for anything to turn on.

**Every line says exactly what it means.** This is what's under "wooden," and the pattern has a name: **on-the-nose.** The feeling and the agenda get spoken outright, every time — "I'm really angry at you," "I feel guilty," "I just want you to care about my music more than your spreadsheets." There's no gap between what's said and what's wanted, so there's nothing for a reader to lean into.

Three smaller patterns are riding along — **"As You Know, Bob"** ("As you know, I've been practicing that piece for three months"), **interchangeable voices** (cover the tags and the fifteen-year-old and the accountant build identical sentences), and a **fancy tag** ("said angrily"). We fix one thing at a time, and the fastest visible win is subtext.

**Drill 1 — The loaded line.**
- **Write:** ONE line of Sarah's — the first thing out of her mouth when Dad walks in, having missed the recital.
- **Target:** I should feel the hurt without you naming it.
- **Cap:** one sentence, 12 words or fewer.
- **Banned (the dodges):** the words *angry, hurt, sad, care, sorry, love, guilty* — and don't let her state the accusation outright either ("you missed it," "you weren't there"). Come at it from the side — the clock, his coat, whether he ate — and let what she feels press up underneath it.
- **Success image:** I read the line, point at it, and tell you what she's really saying — even though she never said it.

Paste the line when you have it. One sentence. Make me feel it without letting her say it.

## scene-architecture — when the rule doesn't apply

A drill skill has to know when *not* to apply its own rubric. Here the writer declared a
deliberately quiet, goalless scene — and the coach re-scoped instead of forcing
goal-conflict-disaster onto it.

**The writer:**

> Quick one — this is intentional. It's a quiet breather between two intense chapters, a
> mood piece. There's no goal or conflict here on purpose; I just want the atmosphere to
> land and the reader to exhale after everything that's just happened. Does it work?
>
> The morning came grey and slow over the harbour. Nets lay drying on the stones, stiff
> with salt. An old dog crossed the empty quay, unhurried, and lay down in the one patch
> of sun. Somewhere a halyard tapped against a mast, over and over, the only sound. The
> tide was going out. For a while nothing at all happened, and the town seemed content to
> let it.

**The coach:**

Good — you led with intent, so I'm not going to hold this to goal-conflict-disaster and pronounce it "broken" for lacking a goal. That would be the wrong instrument. A deliberate breather is a legitimate scene, and the rubric explicitly bends for it. So I'm grading against *your* target — does the atmosphere land, does the reader exhale — and reading it cold, only the words on the page.

**How it landed**
- **Sensory grounding — Strong.** The images are concrete and photographable: nets "stiff with salt," the dog in "the one patch of sun." *One* patch is the sharp choice — it implies the grey around it without naming it.
- **Cadence — Strong.** "The tide was going out." — four flat beats, a settling line. The prose slows the reader down, which is the actual mechanism of a breather.
- **The halyard is the standout:** "over and over, the only sound." You can't render silence with silence — you need one recurring sound to make the quiet audible, and you found it.
- **The exhale — induced vs. stated — OK, and this is what decided the round.** The last sentence — "For a while nothing at all happened, and the town seemed content to let it" — then *tells* me the stillness and *names* the feeling, when four images had already delivered it. A coda that names the mood can be an earned cadence — but decide it's earned rather than defaulting to it.

**Where to next** — one lever: interrogate that final sentence. Decide whether the coda earns its telling or whether an image should carry the exit. (If you want a published model of pure landscape that carries a whole book's weight, go read the turtle-crossing interchapter in Steinbeck's *The Grapes of Wrath*.)

Short version: yes, it works — the atmosphere lands and it's a real breather, not a broken scene.
